Governing the AI Transition Initiative at the School of Government and Policy

Recap: Governing the AI Transition (GAIT) focuses on how liberal democracies can navigate the transition to powerful AI systems—systems that are highly general, that operate autonomously without the need for proximate human oversight, and that are extremely capable. For brevity we sometimes write AGI, Artificial General Intelligence, but do not mean to invoke here any particular vision of superintelligence or human loss of control.

  1. Premises
    1. AGI will be a major social transition.
    2. Social institutions are tools for weathering and thriving through transitions.
    3. Even when functioning well, most institutions of liberal democracy are not fit-to-purpose for an AGI transition.
    4. AGI arrives at a time when many institutions of liberal democracy are not in fact functioning well.
  2. Goal 1: build institutional capacity to govern AI systems that are arriving whether we're ready or not. Success looks like: AI doesn't undermine democracy, rule of law, accountability structures
  3. Goal 2: use AI to repair and strengthen institutions that were already failing before AI arrived. Success looks like: AI actually improves democratic legitimacy, state capacity, epistemic quality of governance.
  4. The design and realization of institutions remade for a world with AGI will require both the inputs of non-computer scientists and the use of AGI itself.
  5. The transition itself will degrade the institutional capacity needed to govern the transition.
  6. The relevant expertise for fixing or replacing those institutions lives outside computer science.
  7. Thinkers outside of computer science can contribute to likelihood that transition advances human well-being
  8. Powerful AI, properly harnessed, can help to draw liberal democracies out of their current economic, political, and social decline

The bootstrapping problem bites differently in each case. For Goal 1, you need functional institutions to regulate AI, but AI is eroding institutional function—so you're trying to fix the plane while flying it. For Goal 2, you need trustworthy AI to repair institutions, but you need functional institutions to ensure AI is trustworthy—a more classically circular dependency. Our goal is to think beyond the parameters of ordinary academic activity to contribute substantively to a transition that advances human well-being, rather than just convening fora where scholars perform for other scholars debating descriptions of problems. We are driven by recognition of the scale of the challenge powerful AI poses to liberal democracies, the dysfunctional state of our existing institutions, and optimism that powerful AI, properly harnessed, can help to draw liberal democracies out of their current economic, political, and social decline.

So we are trying to do two thing? One is address institutional capacity to meet the challenge of AGI. Two is to harness AGI to help institutions transcend their state of decline.

Bootstrapping problem. But a bit like AI regulation requires AI.

Activities

We will help to build the field of sociotechnical alignment as a necessary complement to the work of technical alignment being done by computer scientists. Technical alignment is about steering - getting the system to do what we want it to do. A computer scientist working on alignment wants to know how make AI systems more capable without making them less safe. Sociotechnical alignment, by comparison, is about what the system should do and how it interacts with societal institutions. These latter dimensions of shaping the behavior of powerful AI systems demand insights and expertise from fields that have studied human normative institutional systems such as law, philosophy, sociology, and economics.

Humanity has arrived at the dawn of the AI age with institutions designed for and optimized for and evolved for a world populated by embodied human intelligences, economic and non-economic organizations composed of humans, expertise embodied in individuals who trained by other humans, most human lives governed by explainable and justifiable decisions made by other humans, limitations on movement, cognitive capacity, and lifespan that are finite and comparable across humanity. These institutions are not ready for AGI. They are not ready to do what institutions do: implement guide human coordination and cooperation to improve human wellbeing. Control. Two points: institutions coordinate and control the elements of the communities in which they exist. Institutions are the mechanisms which ensure the coordination and cooperation among the agents in a community.

Sociotechnical Alignment: Shaping the behavior of powerful AI systems is not only an engineering problem. The task demands insights and expertise from fields that have studied human normative systems in depth—such as law, philosophy, and economics. This project develops and deploys concrete technical alignment proposals (including both ex ante (things you can do to build in rules, constraints, values, good behavior before AI is deployed) and ex post (auditing, evaluating, and correcting you can do after an AI is running) implementation techniques) from outside computer science. It also develops infrastructure to support broader public input into system specification documents used for model alignment and evaluations used to assess model and system behavior. We believe it is critical to develop methods that enable democratic institutions and processes to determine

  1. what kind of AI is built,
  2. how it behaves, and
  3. how it interacts with economic, social and political systems. Currently this alignment work is done entirely within private technology companies. DJR: How does this happen? Do we have a blueprint to guide these non-CS thinkers? Or is this just description of G,S, and N research streams?
  • DJR: implementation can include recruiting people who do this and/or identifying scholars who are ripe to transition into this work and give them the cross training necessary to make the jump.
  • Pilot department level workshops to introduce the problem space and the opportunity landscape. Follow up with substantive cross-training.

Institutional Design: Powerful AI will necessitate radical rethinking of existing governing institutions. GAIT will develop institutional proposals calibrated to the constitutional moment that powerful AI will create, revisiting basic questions of how the bureaucracy, judiciary, legislature, executive, and systems of representation should function with and in response to powerful AI and in partnership with possibly reimagined roles for the private sector and markets. Summit idea: Launch institutional proposals with a summit comparing this constitutional moment to past periods of rapid societal change. Two preliminary moves:

  • one idea each for bureaucracy, judiciary, legislature, executive, and systems of representation are implicated in AGI transition
  • catalog 3 candidate comparable past moments of rapid society change (e.g., 18th century democratic revolutions; railroads; electricity; telephony; atomic power/weapons; globalization of trade)
  • GROUP READING OF Bullock et al. 2025 AGI, Governments, and Free Societies

AGI Preparedness: AGI will affect every sector of society. Successfully steering widespread and potentially radical change requires pairing AGI expertise with domain expertise in every relevant domain. This project develops a methodology for vulnerability scanning (identifying latent risks that powerful AI will expose), obstacle mapping (identifying sclerotic systems that will obstruct deployment of beneficial AI systems), and capacity building (actually aiming powerful AI at significant social challenges). Through collaborations with experts across policy domains, it develops a “to-do” list for societal adaptation to powerful AI. Summit idea: Launch a report on AGI preparedness in higher education. First steps: identify best model of already doing this that's out there and what methodological ideas can be derived from it. Refs WEF Global Future Council on Artificial General Intelligence Preparing for Artificial General Intelligence: Global Risks and International Coordination

Real World Evaluations: Just as with the COVID dashboard, the public needs to understand the nature and pace of AI progress, in ways that track real world usage, point AI developers towards the use cases that will be most societally beneficial (since every evaluation becomes an optimization target), and guide governments and regulatory systems. This project develops the science of evaluations and a suite of realistic model evaluations focused on public benefit uses. DJR: "problems worth solving" - multiple dimensions here: deepen public understanding (of what is possible) so democratic priorities can be expressed and push eval science in directions that capture such priorities?

Building State Capacity: Agentic AI could revolutionize sclerotic bureaucracies but in doing so risks radically concentrating power in a few private companies or, indeed, in powerful AI models themselves. GAIT will build a team of “forward-deployed engineers” who can build agent evaluations calibrated to the specific needs of city, state, and federal government officials, and develop scaffolds and reinforcement learning protocols that enable the most capable agentic AI systems to contribute to rebuilding governance capacity. First steps: perhaps morph FDE concept slightly into forward deployed X that captures the applied-to-real-problems-cities-states-countries-need-to-solve-for-the-people.

Ryan on Intelligence Transitions

Human history has traversed several "intelligence transitions" in the past. By intelligence transiton we mean the introduction of a new type of intelligent agent into an existing social order.

Proto-social group life - governed by hierarchy and "pecking order" - gave us tools (e.g., communication, stable expectations, trust, planning) that enabled the formation of the earliest organizations - dyads. The recognition that two weaker individuals could share a mental model, align their objectives, and coordinate against a stronger third party completely disrupted the existing power structure. Individual brains had to learn to map invisible alliances. In a world of dyads and larger collectives we need language for gossip and reputation tracking. We had to invent mechanisms to detect hidden coordination. Our maps of other agents had to include abstractions like "team." We had to create alignment tools like costly signals of loyalty: blood oaths, strict explicit kinship rules, and gift economies. Trust suddenly became a potential weapon so humans had to invent social technologies to verify it. The first time a human recognized that a dyad could conspire against it must have been both exhilarating and terrifying. From the top down direction, members of a group might have learned a sense of "us," a shared awareness of itself as a single, unified agent that could outlive its members. At that moment the individual was suddenly dwarfed. And smaller collectives might suddenly be a threat to the whole. NOTE: the point is not which is prior but that emergence of solidary units generates alignment problems.

The first time one individual collected and embodied all that was known about some topic or skill must have been both exhilarating and terrifying.

In each case we can imagine that there was no real explicit preparation for the transition - most of what happens is reactive and ex post. But can we imagine what that looked like?
