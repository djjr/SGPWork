GAIT › DJR on public input efforts

We suggest "infrastructure to support broader public input" [into model spec docs and evals and assessment]. What is the status quo?

"Infrastructure" is an overly ambitious term for the status quo. Most of what exists is either (a) platforms with interoperability but modest institutional authority (Weval, Global Dialogues, Arena), or (b) modestly institutionalized processes inside particular lab and fully controlled by them (e.g., OpenAI's Collective Alignment).

What doesn't yet exist is public-input infrastructure with independent standing, an entity that can compel adoption, or whose evaluations labs can't simply decline to weight. That gap might be described as an IVO-shaped hole with "public" standing in for "democratic."

Status Quo Examples

A canonical first instance is Anthropic's Collective Constitutional AI (Dec 2023), run with the Collective Intelligence Project. (public input using the Polis platform, sampling ~1,000 U.S. adults who proposed and voted on normative principles, which were then consolidated into a "public constitution" and used to train a model. Clean demonstration of full pipeline: empirical source of principles → train → evaluate, but it was a one-shot research artifact, not infrastructure.

OpenAI's Democratic Inputs to AI grant program (2023), a sort of incubator, funded 10 teams to "design ideas and tools to collectively govern AI" and open-sourced their code, and prototypes. Important finding: combining AI-assisted deliberation with non-AI steps (e.g., expert curation of clauses, a final human vote) was perceived as more trustworthy and legitimate than full automation.

Weval is the flagship: an open, community-driven evaluation platform, pitched explicitly as a "Wikipedia for evaluations" that lets anybody build and run their own AI evaluations. Evaluations are published as rubrics of prompts and criteria, and the platform auto-translates uploaded evaluations into a standardized format.

Global Dialogues is a recurring multi-country surveys tracking public attitudes toward AI across 70+ countries. It's designed so evaluations can be built on top of it. Has been used by Meta, Cohere, Taiwan's Ministry of Digital Affairs, and the UK and US AI Safety Institutes to stress-test assumptions and shape evaluation agendas. Collective Intelligence Project describes it as a closed loop: public input → shared norms → evaluations → model behavior → renewed input.

Public red-teaming is an older, more adversarial form of public input. The DEF CON AI Village Generative Red Team Challenge (2023) was organized via Humane Intelligence (Rumman Chowdhury) and SeedAI. Not really infrastructure as these are episodic events drawing a self-selected hacker public, not standing institutions.

The Arena line of work is high volume public-input-into-evaluation infrastructure where millions of pairwise human preference votes on model outputs to prompts feed public leaderboards. The modality is aggregated revealed preference rather than deliberation or rubric-authoring. It illustrates a Goodhart's law pathology of public input: once a leaderboard becomes a target, labs optimize for it; evals become training-signal.

Epistemology and Power

"Public input into specifications" today largely means input into the negotiable middle of the spec, not its fundamental constraints. OpenAI's Collective Alignment document states explicitly that "[w]hile public input will be valuable across all levels of the instruction hierarchy, changes will be less likely to be adopted the higher up the hierarchy they go, especially at the platform-level." They distinguish some crowd feedback leading to "clarifications" and some to "change-of-principles."

As in all participatory input/choice processes, the boundary around what is choose-able is where things get interesting.

OpenAI's August 2025 Collective Alignment update candidly illustrates three classical faces of agenda setting power familiar to social scientists (three dimensions, we might say, of the often too glibly used "Overton window"). The first face, the visible layer where input is permitted, shows in what was adopted: a wording clarification allowing broad-audience political content, the negotiable surface of the Spec. The second face corresponds to Bachrach and Baratz's (1970) "nondecision-making decision-making," control of the agenda rather than the vote. OpenAI admits this outright: participants never reviewed the Spec, only ranked responses to prompts OpenAI "pre-selected," and the company grants that "the prompts we pre-selected for input shaped the feedback." The choice set was authored upstream of participation. The third face is Lukes's (1974) power over what people take themselves to want. It appears where crowd majorities were refused: on tailored political content, OpenAI says that "it was unclear that participants had considered these risks," reserving the authority to discount a preference as under-informed. Public input is thus scoped to defaults in developer-selected, value-sensitive domains; where it pressed on boundaries (political targeting, erotica), it was "set aside based on principle or feasibility" in internal review. Cf Schattschneider (1960): some issues are organized in, others organized out.

"Public input" into system specification for model alignment and evaluations to assess it falls short of broad democratic control of AI futures in the same way that democratic control is attenuated any time the menu is fixed before a choice is made. Many fields have named this independently: the choice set / feasible set / opportunity set / budget set in economics, the menu in social choice, the message or strategy space in mechanism design, the action space in reinforcement learning, and the hypothesis space, inductive bias, and hyperparameters in machine learning, the Overton window (1990s) and agenda-setting in political-communication.

Outtake

One dimension of criticism of public input efforts is that it tends to center around a model purveyor soliciting tweaks rather than permitting the public to "query what is under the hood." Our ex ante / ex post framing (perhaps via IVO type approaches?) might be a move beyond this.
