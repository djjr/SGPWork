Thinking in Clusters (Themes)

To make the visualization we had to think across courses and identify thematic clusters. Claude's initial take was this:

  • Democracy & Legitimacy
  • Economy & Labor
  • Evaluation Science
  • Risk & Safety
  • Institutions & the State
  • Model Internals After some back and forths we added two others:
  • Normative & Political Foundations
  • Design & Synthesis Each outcome was then assigned to zero or more of these clusters.

Sometimes it makes sense to try to construct a coherent narrative about a set of learning outcomes. I asked Claude to think about this for Risk and Safety. Here's what it came up with:

The Risk & Safety cluster cultivates a single capacity: the ability to reason rigorously about harm under deep uncertainty — to anticipate it, detect it, allocate it, and contain it — across timescales that run from an acute crisis to a slow erosion of the things people value. It begins by fixing the terrain of harm at both ends of that range, pairing the sharp, concentrated dangers of war, geopolitics, and physical infrastructure (#SecurityAndGeopolitics) with the diffuse, gradual pressures on culture, relationships, science, and long-run flourishing (#CultureScienceFlourishing). Against that map it teaches the central analytic move of the field — separating opportunity from risk and asking not merely whether a system is dangerous but for whom (#WinnersLosers). Detection comes next: building early-warning evaluations that surface catastrophic propensities before they mature (#EarlyWarning), tempered at once by the humility to see why those very tools may fail as systems grow more capable (#ScalingLimits). Where a risk cannot be removed at its source — misuse, or harms emerging from uncoordinated action — the response shifts to hardening the target rather than fixing the model (#HardenTargets). Underwriting all of it is a reckoning with the deepest stakes, the normative weight of losing control and of what we owe future generations (#LongHorizon), which is what lifts this cluster above ordinary risk management.

We then have an exchange where I ask it to be critical of what we have so far. "Given what we SHOULD mean by "risk and safety (of AI/AGI)", are we missing anything fundamental? Are there things that are implicit here that should probably be explicit?"

Claude thinks there are.

"Reading it back with a cold eye, the honest headline is this: as drafted, the cluster is closer to "AI risk impacts and their assessment" than to "risk and safety" in the sense the term carries for AGI. It's strong on cataloguing harms (#SecurityAndGeopolitics, #CultureScienceFlourishing, #WinnersLosers) and on evaluation (#EarlyWarning, #ScalingLimits), but the distinctively safety half — why advanced systems become dangerous, and the repertoire for preventing it — is thin, mostly implicit, or quietly outsourced to other clusters. If you take the field's own center of gravity to be the alignment problem, loss of control, catastrophic misuse, and structural/systemic risk, then several of those are missing here as explicit objects.

The genuinely fundamental gaps:

  • The alignment problem itself. Nowhere does an outcome say "understand why getting a highly capable system to reliably do what we intend is an unsolved problem" (specification gaming, goal misgeneralization, inner vs. outer alignment). #ScalingLimits presupposes it — you can't ask why alignment techniques fail to scale without alignment as a premise — but the premise is never stated. That's a hollow center for a safety cluster.

  • Loss of control as a mechanism, not just a stake. #LongHorizon treats "loss of control" as a normative question (what we owe future generations). But the empirical, technical study of how an autonomous system could slip control — instrumental convergence, deceptive alignment, self-exfiltration, autonomous replication — is absent. For AGI specifically this is the most conspicuous hole: the cluster contemplates the ethics of losing control without teaching the mechanics of it.

  • A threat-model taxonomy. The field organizes around misuse vs. misalignment vs. structural/systemic risk. All three are lurking here — #HardenTargets names "misuse" and "uncoordinated action," the evaluation outcomes gesture at misalignment — but they're scattered and never named as a framework. Making that taxonomy explicit would give the cluster the spine it currently lacks.

  • Concentration of power / structural risk / gradual disempowerment. A first-class safety concern — and one GAIT's own proposal cares about ("radically concentrating power in a few private companies or in the models themselves") — that simply isn't here. #WinnersLosers handles distribution of harm but not the specific risk of irreversible power concentration or slow human disempowerment.

  • The response repertoire beyond hardening society. #HardenTargets is the cluster's only mitigation outcome, and tellingly it's the mitigation for risks you can't fix at the source. That quietly presupposes source-fixing — alignment, AI control/containment, defense-in-depth, dangerous-capability thresholds and if-then commitments, safety cases — happens somewhere. Some of it does live elsewhere (#AlignmentToolkit, the Institutions cluster), but a safety cluster that contains only the residual response is oddly shaped.

Two things that are implicit and should be made explicit even if you add nothing:

The narrative leans on "reasoning under deep uncertainty," but no outcome actually teaches risk-assessment method — severity × likelihood, tail/heavy-tailed risk, decision-making under deep uncertainty, precaution vs. cost. #EarlyWarning is detection, not assessment. And there's an unresolved scope question: the cluster straddles diffuse present harms (#CultureScienceFlourishing) and catastrophic/existential futures (#LongHorizon, #EarlyWarning) without ever declaring whether "risk and safety" here means all harm or catastrophic harm — a distinction the field fights about (AI ethics vs. AI safety), and one your students will notice if it's left ambient.

The fair counterpoint, so this doesn't read as "add everything": some of these legitimately belong to other clusters — alignment technique to Model Internals/Eval, governance response to Institutions, power to Economy. So the real question is a design decision, not a checklist. Is Risk & Safety meant to be the conceptual home of threat models + the alignment problem + loss of control — in which case it needs two or three new outcomes (a threat-taxonomy outcome, a loss-of-control/agentic-risk outcome, and a risk-assessment-method outcome) — or is it the "impacts + evaluation" node, with safety-as-response distributed, in which case the honest move is to rename it (e.g., "Risk Impacts & Evaluation") and add explicit cross-references so the missing center is visibly located elsewhere rather than merely absent?

Which of those two is it supposed to be? That answer determines whether we patch the cluster or relabel it."

I reply that I'll have to think about this. I then ask Claude to write each outcome into a separate md file in my Obsidian vault. These are the files linked to in the text above.

KNOLs

A long time ago Google considered challenging Wikipedia for cataloging all knowledge and came up with the concept of KNOL to describe a sort of "molecule of knowledge." I like to use this concept to describe the concrete bits of knowledge or skill or know-how that are components of learning outcomes. These are the things that we have to concretely insert into our learning materials and activities and assessments. I ask Claude to draft a few and we iterate for a while to come up with some examples. For #ModelAnatomy, we have:

  • Token & tokenization (text ⇄ tokens)
  • Embedding vector (token → vector)
  • Parameter / weight (what "learned" means)
  • Self-attention (query–key–value, conceptually)
  • The transformer block, and stacking depth
  • Context window / sequence length
  • Next-token prediction as the objective
  • Parameter count and what "scaling" refers to

GAPS - what is missing in this curriculum?

I asked Claude to take a hard look at everything we have so far and to identify high level gaps. I ask "If we are trying to education public policy professionals who are prepared to make a difference as we approach the problem of how to govern the A(G)I transition, what have we not considered?" Claude responds by writing an extensive MPP AI Gap Analysis. Here are its major observations.

Part 1 — Structural gaps (missing whole territories)

Gap 1: International and geopolitical governance ⭐ biggest gap

The curriculum assesses geopolitics as an impact (#SecurityAndGeopolitics, C1) but never teaches the international governance toolkit. Every governance instrument taught (C3, C5) is domestic. Yet the defining features of the AGI governance problem — race dynamics between states and labs, compute as a controllable input, verification of agreements, the fate of countries with no frontier developer — are international. A graduate who can design a regulatory market but can't reason about export controls or treaty verification is unprepared for roughly half the field.

Proposed outcomes (as a new course, or a major C3 expansion):

  1. #ComputeGovernance — Analyze compute-based policy levers (export controls, chip tracking, cloud KYC (know your customer), verification regimes) as instruments of international AI governance. (Seam: builds directly on Core #ComputeStack, which maps the chokepoints but doesn't treat them as levers.)
  2. #RaceDynamics — Model strategic dynamics among states and labs (racing, deterrence, coordination failure) and assess interventions intended to alter them.
  3. #TreatyDesign — Evaluate proposals for international AI agreements and institutions (monitoring, verification, benefit-sharing) against the track record of arms-control and nonproliferation regimes.
  4. #GlobalStakes — Assess how the transition distributes power and benefit across countries, including those with no frontier developers. (Extends #WinnersLosers, which stops at "populations and regions.")

Gap 2: The politics of getting anything adopted

The curriculum teaches students to design governance (#DesignGov, #SectorPlan, #NewMechanisms) but not to win. Nothing addresses power, interests, coalitions, framing, policy windows, or capture. C3's prose mentions "lobbyists/corporate government strategy" as a tool to survey, but no outcome makes political feasibility a skill. This is the classic failure mode of governance education: graduates who can draft the perfect mechanism and cannot move it one inch. AI sharpens the problem — the regulated industry holds an extreme information advantage and much of the relevant expertise.

Proposed outcomes (fit in C3, or a politics-focused course; see Part 3):

  1. #PowerMap — Map the interests, resources, and influence channels of the actors in a specific AI policy fight (labs, incumbents, civil society, publics, agencies).
  2. #CaptureRisk — Diagnose regulatory capture and industry–government information asymmetry in AI policy, and design safeguards against them.
  3. #WindowWatch — Analyze how policy windows open (incidents, elections, crises) and construct a strategy for moving a proposal when one opens.
  4. #AdoptionPath — Construct a feasible adoption path — coalition, framing, venue, sequencing — for a governance design produced in #DesignGov or #SectorPlan.

Gap 3: Deciding and acting under deep uncertainty, including crisis

C1 teaches forecasting (#Foresight, #ForecastAudit) — epistemics, not decision-making. Nothing teaches how to choose when timelines are contested, error costs are asymmetric, and some mistakes are irreversible. And nothing anywhere covers crisis governance: #EarlyWarning (C4) detects the alarm; no outcome answers it. If the transition is fast, graduates will govern in emergency conditions.

Proposed outcomes:

  1. #ActUnderUncertainty — Apply decision frameworks for deep uncertainty (precaution, option value, irreversibility, cost-of-delay vs. cost-of-error) to a contested AI policy choice. (C1 or C5.)
  2. #CrisisPlaybook — Design an incident-response mechanism for AI emergencies (frontier-model incident, large-scale misuse event): triggers, authorities, coordination, communication. (C5; pairs with #HardenTargets.)
  3. #ExpertDisagreement — Adjudicate among conflicting expert worldviews (fast/slow timelines, catastrophic/mundane risk) without collapsing into either deference or dismissal. (C1; deepens #ForecastAudit.)

Gap 4: The actual, existing legal-regulatory landscape

C3 treats "legal systems" at the theory level; no outcome requires knowing what already exists: the EU AI Act, US executive and state action, China's provisions, sectoral regulators, standards bodies (NIST, ISO), liability doctrine. Graduates will walk into offices where the first question is "how does this interact with current law?"

Proposed outcomes (C3, or split with C5):

  1. #RegMap — Map the current AI regulatory landscape across major jurisdictions and compare the mechanism designs embedded in each.
  2. #LiabilityGaps — Analyze how existing doctrine (tort, IP, privacy, antitrust, agency law) applies — and fails to apply — to AI systems and autonomous agents. (Feeds C3 #NewMechanisms: agent ID/registration is partly a response to these gaps.)

Gap 5: Present-tense algorithmic government

The curriculum is frontier-focused and skips the deployed present: algorithmic decision systems already inside government (benefits, policing, hiring), the documented harms literature (bias, discrimination, surveillance), and the accountability toolkit built for it (impact assessments, audits, procurement rules, redress). Two reasons this matters: many graduates' first jobs are here, and this constituency's politics shape what frontier governance is possible.

Proposed outcomes:

  1. #GovAI — Evaluate a government deployment of an algorithmic or AI system across procurement, impact assessment, audit, and redress. (C5; concretizes #AdminState.)
  2. #EverydayHarms — Analyze documented harms of deployed AI systems (bias, discrimination, surveillance) and assess the accountability mechanisms developed in response. (C1 or C3.)

Part 2 — Course-level additions (smaller, targeted)

Core — Building the AI Frontier

  • #LabGovernance — Assess corporate-governance instruments at frontier labs (RSPs/frontier safety policies, board structures, safety cases, whistleblower channels) as a layer of AI governance. (Could instead deepen C3 #SoftGovernance, which names the category but not these instruments. Given #WhoControlsAI, Core is the natural home: the labs are currently the de facto governors.)

C1 — Tracking and Anticipating AI Impacts

  • #EnviroFootprint — (candidate) Assess the energy, water, and land-use impacts of the compute build-out and the policy conflicts they create. (Prose says "every area"; this one is conspicuously absent and politically live.)

C2 — AI and What We Owe to Each Other

  • #InsiderEthics — Reason about role morality for professionals inside the transition: dissent, whistleblowing, complicity, and the ethics of working within institutions one believes inadequate. (The course covers what society owes; not what the graduate, in seat, owes. For this degree's actual alumni, arguably the most load-bearing normative question.)

C3 — Building the Governance Frontier

  • #TechPrecedents — Extract design lessons, including failure modes, from past attempts to govern transformative technologies (nuclear, recombinant DNA/Asilomar, internet, financial innovation). (#GovernanceRoots traces governance-in-general; the nearest analogical evidence base for AI governance goes unmentioned.)

C4 — Evaluating and Steering AI

  • Largely complete for its remit. One addition:
  • #SecurityOfModels — (candidate) Explain model security as distinct from alignment: theft of weights, jailbreak robustness, safeguards against fine-tuning away safety training. (A steering technique that fails against an adversary isn't a steering technique.)

C5 — Societal Adaptation to Advanced AI

  • #CrisisPlaybook and #GovAI (Part 1, above) both land here.

Part 3 — Program-level gaps (not fixable inside any one course)

Working with AI, not just about it

No outcome anywhere requires a student to use a frontier system. The curriculum studies AI the way one might study a distant volcano. Policy professionals who haven't pushed a frontier model to its limits will systematically misjudge both capabilities and failure modes — and will be out-analyzed by those who have. The GAIT memo's "AI upskilling salons" concede the point for DC professionals; the degree students need it more.

  • #FluentPractitioner — Use frontier AI tools critically in one's own policy work (analysis, drafting, red-teaming) and evaluate the reliability of their outputs.

Cheapest implementation: not a new course but a thread — every course requires at least one assignment where AI use is mandatory, disclosed, and critiqued.

Translation across the technical–political boundary

The degree's whole premise is that its graduates bridge technical and policy worlds — but no outcome names the bridging act. AI-specific (so not delegable to the core MPP): communicating eval results, capability claims, and genuine uncertainty to non-technical decision-makers without hype or false precision.

  • #TranslateTech — Communicate technical claims about AI systems, including their uncertainty, to non-technical decision-makers in briefing, testimony, and memo form. (Natural summative pairing: brief a mock committee on a #BuildAnEval result.)

Synthesis / capstone

Six courses, no outcome that forces integration. #Trajectory (C1) synthesizes within one course. Nothing requires combining technical judgment (Core, C4), normative argument (C2), institutional design (C3), and adaptation strategy (C5) on one real problem.

Building the AI Frontier: From Linear Algebra to Political Economy

Artificial Intelligence is already the driving force animating the US economy; its societal impact is likely to be orders of magnitude greater still. This course introduces students to the central concepts of artificial intelligence, particularly those underpinning the current AI transition. Students will learn about the different stages of training a large AI model, the paradigms within which rapid AI progress has been made, and the developments that may drive future advances. As well as developing a basic familiarity with the architecture of a frontier AI system, students will learn about their material foundations: the humans, hardware, supply chains, infrastructure and capital flows that make it possible to train vast AI models at an expense of billions of dollars.

Learning outcomes: TrainingPipeline · ModelAnatomy · ScalingDrivers · ComputeStack · WhoControlsAI · HypeVsReal · MathFoundations

Courses

Tracking and Anticipating AI Impacts

Current AI systems are affecting every area of the economy and society; still more capable systems in the near future may prove even more transformative. Tracking and anticipating these impacts requires drawing on a wide range of social scientific methodologies, as well as developing new ones for anticipating and steering what's on the horizon. This course will explore the current and prospective impacts—both opportunities and risks—of frontier AI systems across a range of societal sectors, focusing on themes such as AI's impacts on democracy and state capacity, on work, the economy, and quality of life, on critical infrastructure including our information ecosystem, on culture, family and relationships, on scientific progress, war and geopolitics, and the long term flourishing of humanity. 

Learning outcomes: MeasureNow · Foresight · EconomyAndWork · DemocracyAndInfo · SecurityAndGeopolitics · CultureScienceFlourishing · WinnersLosers · ForecastAudit · Trajectory

AI and What We Owe to Each Other

All technological progress changes the content of our obligations to one another, as new harms and benefits become possible; only rarely does technological progress change the grounds of those obligations, the reasons why they obtain. This course exposes students to the uncharted normative horizon exposed by rapid AI progress. They will learn to identify and interrogate both novel practical questions, like how courteous an autonomous vehicle should be and what actions or decisions can appropriately be delegated to an AI agent, and fundamental challenges about whether the decline of mutual interdependence undermines the foundations of the social contract that lies at the heart of the liberal tradition, or how we should think about the prospect of humans, in some sense, losing control of humanity's future. 

Learning outcomes: ContentVsGrounds · NewDilemmas · SocialContract · FrameworksClash · LongHorizon · MakeTheCase · DoWeOweAI

Building the Governance Frontier

In this course we will go back to the foundations of collective governing across the evolutionary history of humans to lay the groundwork for thinking about how the next phases of governance might be built to effectively ensure human flourishing through the AI transition. We will explore the theory of normative social order, markets, democracy, capitalism, socialism, and other methods of governance through human history. We revisit today's tools including legal systems, legal infrastructure, legislative bodies, courts, local communities, civic organizations, lobbyists/corporate government strategy, self-governance, market/industry organizations and standard-setting, and the role of epistemic communities. We’ll then explore innovative proposals for governance methods such as regulatory markets and the need for novel infrastructure for AI agents that link these agents to existing governance institutions. Our goal will be to lay the groundwork for true innovation in new methods of governance that are adapted to the scale, speed and complexity of AI while remaining true to the core ideals of democratic governance.

Learning outcomes: GovernanceRoots · WaysToOrder · GovToolkit · NewMechanisms · DemocraticTest · DesignGov · SoftGovernance

Evaluating and Steering AI

This course will introduce students to the technical tools used to steer AI for the better, focusing in particular on methods for evaluating AI systems' capabilities and propensities, and techniques intended to tune those propensities towards safety and societal benefit. Students will learn about evaluations that explore AI behaviors "in the wild" as well as more static evaluations in the lab, focusing in particular on "early warning systems" intended to expose propensities that, in more capable models, could be catastrophically harmful. They will explore the strengths and limitations of current "alignment" methods such as reinforcement learning from human feedback, and from verifiable rewards, and system-level defences such as input and output filters, and chain of thought monitoring. And they will explore early attempts to develop alignment techniques that could work for much more capable systems and that are more responsive to democratic values and processes.  

Learning outcomes: CapabilityVsPropensity · BuildAnEval · EarlyWarning · AlignmentToolkit · ScalingLimits · DemocraticAlignment · OpenTheBox
