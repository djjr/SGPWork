Curriculum Design and Employer Demand.md

Consider this 2024 post by folks at [MATS](https://matsprogram.org/) on Less Wrong: "Talent Needs of Technical AI Safety Teams"

Claude reflections based on prompt to think about it in relation to GAIT and SGP curriculum. Distillation, filtered for "what can GAIT do that isn't MATS."

The article's core findings, in brief: The field sorts talent into three archetypes

  • Connectors (conceptual bridge-builders)
  • Iterators (high-throughput empiricists who build tight feedback loops)
  • Amplifiers (communicators/managers who multiply others' impact). Demand is Iterators first, Amplifiers rising fast as orgs hit 10–30 FTE, and Connectors oversupplied at the junior level ("there are a lot of 'idea guys' who don't actually have very good ideas"). MATS selects people who are already 90% there because 10 weeks can't build a researcher from scratch.

Ideas for GAIT:

  1. Occupy the pipeline stage MATS explicitly doesn't serve. MATS's "we select the 90%-done" admission is a structural opening. Someone has to produce that 90%. A degree-length program is the wrong instrument for MATS's job (final polish + placement) but the right one for the slow-build job. Don't compete on the last mile; own the first ten.

  2. Train Amplifiers deliberately — nobody else is. The article says Amplifier (communicators/managers who multiply others' impact) demand is unmet, their development is the most tractable of the three, and the field's current answer is "hope they arrive from industry with soft skills and then acquire AI safety context somehow." A program that pairs management/communication/project-craft with real technical fluency (enough to read papers, run evals, sanity-check claims) would produce a profile the field wants and MATS structurally can't make. This dovetails with the translation-across-the-technical-political-boundary gap our MPP-AI analysis flagged; the Amplifier is the technical-org version of that same bridging role.

    One caution worth designing around: strong opinions/vision and collaborative skills like compromise seem anticorrelated. Teaching students to hold views loosely while working inside someone else's agenda is itself a trainable skill.

  3. Teach feedback-loop construction as an explicit subject. The Iterator's defining skill isn't ML knowledge — it's building tight experimental loops, making predictions before running things, and using frictional time well. Universities teach content; almost nobody teaches research throughput as a craft. Assignments that grade the loop (prediction → experiment → update → next experiment, repeatedly, under time pressure) rather than the final artifact would develop this. The article also notes the field's screening proxies — output volume at controlled quality, speed at fixed accuracy — so students should graduate with legible throughput evidence (public repos, replication logs), not just grades.

  4. Structure the Connector path as discourse, not curriculum. The article is emphatic that Connector ability develops only through years of arguing with a robust network of interlocutors ("you only get better at reasoning about AI safety by reasoning about AI safety") and that one-size-fits-all education doesn't help. GAIT can't shortcut this, but it can institutionalize the precondition: standing adversarial-collaboration seminars, required public write-ups that invite field criticism, and imported practitioners as regular sparring partners. Treat this as infrastructure for the few who'll become Connectors. Heed the warning about junior-Connector glut: route most students toward Iterator/Amplifier skills first.

  5. Steal the negative pedagogical findings. MATS ran the experiment for you: mandatory programming actively depressed reported value; optional programming decays after early weeks and has a ceiling; the durable high-value activities were research and networking, full stop. For GAIT this argues for a thin required core, most learning organized around a real research or client project, and spending program resources on connecting students to the field rather than on more content hours.

  6. Keep one eye on the streetlight. The most-upvoted substantive comment thread (TsviBT) argues the whole report optimizes for on-paradigm, prosaic work and that the field risks producing people who can only work under the streetlight. The report's own hedge: this prioritization "only holds at this moment." Future needs hard to predict. A curriculum should teach students to recognize which problems current methods can't touch, even while training them in the employable, on-paradigm skills.

GAIT's comparative advantage is the slow, upstream work MATS can't do: build technically-fluent Amplifiers and throughput-trained proto-Iterators over years; borrow MATS's hard-won lesson that projects and networks teach, and mandatory content mostly doesn't.

Sources: Talent Needs of Technical AI Safety Teams; GAIT knowledge doc "MPP-AI Curriculum Gaps"
