MPP-AI Curriculum — Open Threads / Parking Lot

Running list of deferred decisions and ideas from the learning-outcomes work. Add to the top.

1. "Knols" — a sub-outcome layer of knowledge/skill atoms

Bookmarked 2026-07-11.

Idea (DJR): beneath each learning outcome sits a finer layer of atomic knowledge/skill units — knols (after Google Knol, ~2007–12, "a unit of knowledge"; DJR's sense: molecularize knowledge). An outcome is a capacity ("evaluate early-warning evaluation strategies"); a knol is a discrete thing you must know or be able to do to have that capacity ("what a propensity eval is," "the idea of a capability threshold," "how to read an ROC curve").

Why it matters / where it leads:

  • Dependencies live here. Two outcomes overlap because they share knols; Course B presumes a knol that Course A teaches. Knol-level mapping makes the overlap/dependency analysis precise instead of vibes-based.
  • Two-tier structure. The current network graph is the coarse (outcome) layer; knols are the fine layer. Same overlap/dependency machinery, finer resolution.
  • Bridges to the rest of course design. Knols map onto readings, lecture units, and assessment items far more naturally than whole outcomes do.

Design decisions (2026-07-11)

Separate the two relations by level. A knol can relate to an outcome two ways — as a component (part-of) or as a prerequisite (drawn-on). Don't overload one structure for both:

  • Composition lives inside an outcome. A knol is a component of exactly one outcome → the knol tree is a strict, single-owner hierarchy (every knol has one home).
  • Prerequisite lives between outcomes. Shared/foundational knowledge is not modeled as a knol shared across outcomes; instead it is owned by a foundational outcome, and dependents point at it with a directed requires edge. That edge is the same overlap/dependency relation the network graph already uses, one layer down and with direction.

Foundational LOs hold the shared substrate — and mostly already exist.

  • Technical side: the Core course is the foundations layer (TrainingPipeline, ModelAnatomy, ScalingDrivers, ComputeStack, …). E.g. "supervised learning" is a knol of TrainingPipeline; downstream outcomes just require it.
  • Governance/normative side (the mirror DJR predicted): GovernanceRoots and WaysToOrder are the foundational outcomes (they resisted clustering because they're substrate), with Ethics primitives ContentVsGrounds / FrameworksClash playing the same role.
  • So the task is less "build foundational LOs" than "name a tier."

Add a tier field to each outcome: foundational / core / applied.

  • Promotion rule: something becomes a foundational LO only if (a) several downstream outcomes depend on it and (b) it's substantial enough to teach as its own capacity. Below that bar it stays a knol under its natural owner + a requires-edge. (This is the guard against foundational-LO proliferation.)
  • Payoff for the graph: tiers give a vertical prerequisite axis (foundations at base → applied/Design & Synthesis at top, edges flowing up) — more legible than the flat force layout, and it explains what the Design & Synthesis cluster is (the top tier).

Knol conventions. Title = a self-contained noun phrase (becomes a note title + [[wikilink]]; so "Supervised learning," not "Supervised"). Keep a consistent grain: one teachable/assessable idea per knol ("token," not "Basics of AI history").

A third category: course framings / motifs. Some items are neither knol nor LO but a through-line a course argues (e.g. "AI as automation," "every eval becomes an optimization target"). Give courses a themes/motifs slot; don't force motifs into the knol tree.

2. Risk & Safety cluster — scope decision (DJR researching)

Raised 2026-07-11.

Critique surfaced that the cluster reads more like "risk impacts + evaluation" than "risk and safety" in the AGI sense. Distinctively-safety content is thin or implicit: the alignment problem itself, loss-of-control as a mechanism (not just a normative stake), a threat-model taxonomy (misuse / misalignment / structural), power concentration & gradual disempowerment, and the response repertoire beyond #HardenTargets (alignment, AI control/containment, defense-in-depth, capability thresholds / if-then commitments, safety cases). Also missing an explicit risk-assessment method (severity × likelihood, tail risk, decision under deep uncertainty).

The decision (DJR to think/research): is Risk & Safety the conceptual home of threat models + alignment problem + loss of control (→ add ~3 outcomes), or the "impacts + evaluation" node with safety-as-response distributed (→ rename, e.g. "Risk Impacts & Evaluation," + explicit cross-references to where the center lives)? This choice determines patch-vs-relabel.
