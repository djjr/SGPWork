MPP-AI — Learning Outcomes (First Draft)

Draft learning outcomes for the MPP-AI concentration, one set per course. Written to support a downstream analysis of overlap (the same content taught in two courses) and dependency (one course assuming what another builds).

Conventions

  • Verb-first, measurable. Each outcome is one cognitive action (explain, analyze, design, evaluate…) applied to a specific content anchor. No "Students will be able to…" boilerplate — same content, less padding.
  • Hashtag handles. Every outcome has a unique #Tag so it can be referenced as an atomic unit in the overlap/dependency matrix. Recurring nouns across tags are intentional — they mark the seams to inspect.
  • Counts are deliberately uneven. This iteration is liberal: survey-style courses (esp. Tracking and Anticipating AI Impacts) carry more outcomes than tightly bounded ones. Normalize by count if you compute per-course overlap.
  • (candidate) marks an outcome that may be deliberately out of scope — flagged for a decision, not yet committed.

Course 0 (Core) — Building the AI Frontier: From Linear Algebra to Political Economy

  1. #TrainingPipeline — Explain the stages of training a frontier model (pretraining, fine-tuning, RLHF/RLVR) and how each stage shapes a model's capabilities and propensities.
  2. #ModelAnatomy — Describe, conceptually, the architecture of a transformer-based system (tokens, parameters, attention, scaling) without requiring prior ML coursework.
  3. #ScalingDrivers — Identify the drivers of recent progress (scaling laws, compute, data, algorithms) and reason about what might drive or stall future advances.
  4. #ComputeStack — Map the material supply chain of frontier AI (compute/hardware, energy, data, capital, labor) and locate its chokepoints.
  5. #WhoControlsAI — Analyze how the political economy of frontier AI (capital intensity, market concentration) shapes who controls the technology and its direction.
  6. #HypeVsReal — Interpret quantitative claims about model cost and capability with enough technical literacy to distinguish a real result from marketing.
  7. #MathFoundations — (candidate) Work hands-on through the core linear-algebra and probability intuitions (vectors, matrix multiplication, gradient descent, distributions over tokens) that underlie how a model computes.

Course 1 — Tracking and Anticipating AI Impacts

  1. #MeasureNow — Apply social-scientific methods, and state their limits, to measure the current impacts of AI across sectors.
  2. #Foresight — Construct and critique methods for anticipating the impacts of more capable systems (forecasting, scenario analysis, trend extrapolation).
  3. #EconomyAndWork — Assess AI's impacts on labor markets, productivity, the economy, and quality of life.
  4. #DemocracyAndInfo — Assess AI's impacts on democracy, state capacity, and the information ecosystem.
  5. #SecurityAndGeopolitics — Assess AI's impacts on war, geopolitics, and physical critical infrastructure.
  6. #CultureScienceFlourishing — Assess AI's impacts on culture, family, relationships, scientific progress, and long-term human flourishing.
  7. #WinnersLosers — Distinguish opportunities from risks and characterize how each is distributed across populations and regions.
  8. #ForecastAudit — Evaluate the reliability of impact claims and forecasts, naming the dominant sources of uncertainty.
  9. #Trajectory — Synthesize cross-sector findings into a coherent account of the transition's likely trajectory.

Course 2 — AI and What We Owe to Each Other

  1. #ContentVsGrounds — Distinguish the content of our obligations from their grounds, and explain how AI changes each differently.
  2. #NewDilemmas — Analyze novel practical normative questions raised by AI (delegation to agents, responsibility gaps, machine "courtesy").
  3. #SocialContract — Evaluate whether declining mutual interdependence erodes the social-contract foundations of the liberal tradition.
  4. #FrameworksClash — Apply major ethical frameworks to concrete AI dilemmas and identify where they conflict or run out.
  5. #LongHorizon — Reason carefully about long-horizon normative questions (loss of control, obligations to future generations).
  6. #MakeTheCase — Construct and defend a normative argument bearing on a specific AI governance choice.
  7. #DoWeOweAI — (candidate) Assess whether, and what, we might owe to AI systems themselves (moral status and patienthood).

Course 3 — Building the Governance Frontier

  1. #GovernanceRoots — Trace the evolutionary and historical development of collective-governance mechanisms and the coordination problems each solved.
  2. #WaysToOrder — Compare governance modalities (markets, democracy, hierarchy, standard-setting, epistemic communities) by how each produces social order.
  3. #GovToolkit — Inventory the contemporary governance toolkit (legal, legislative, judicial, civic, market/industry) and assess each tool's fit for AI.
  4. #NewMechanisms — Analyze innovative proposals (regulatory markets, agent ID/registration, infrastructure linking agents to institutions) against AI's scale, speed, and complexity.
  5. #DemocraticTest — Evaluate governance designs against democratic ideals: legitimacy, accountability, representation.
  6. #DesignGov — Design a governance mechanism for a specific AI problem and justify its institutional fit.
  7. #SoftGovernance — Distinguish private/self-governance, industry standard-setting, and epistemic communities as governance from state/hard governance.

Course 4 — Evaluating and Steering AI

  1. #CapabilityVsPropensity — Explain how AI evaluations work and distinguish capability evaluations from propensity evaluations.
  2. #BuildAnEval — Compare in-the-wild and static/lab evaluations, and design an evaluation for a given capability or propensity.
  3. #EarlyWarning — Assess "early-warning" evaluation strategies for propensities that could be catastrophic in more capable models.
  4. #AlignmentToolkit — Explain and critique current alignment methods (RLHF, RLVR) and system-level defenses (I/O filters, chain-of-thought monitoring).
  5. #ScalingLimits — Analyze why current techniques may fail to scale to more capable systems.
  6. #DemocraticAlignment — Evaluate approaches for making alignment and evaluation responsive to democratic values and processes.
  7. #OpenTheBox — Explain interpretability and transparency methods for understanding model internals and behavior.

Course 5 — Societal Adaptation to Advanced AI

  1. #SystemVsSociety — Distinguish system-directed interventions (aligning/governing the model) from institution-directed "societal adaptation" interventions.
  2. #ClearThePath — Identify norms and regulations that block beneficial AI deployment and propose targeted reforms.
  3. #HardenTargets — Analyze risks that cannot be mitigated at the system level (misuse, byproducts of uncoordinated action) and design target-hardening responses.
  4. #AdminState — Assess how the administrative state must adapt to advanced AI.
  5. #EconomyAdapts — Assess how economic institutions (labor, distribution, markets) must adapt to advanced AI.
  6. #DemocracyAdapts — Assess how democratic institutions must adapt to advanced AI.
  7. #AdaptationTradeoffs — Evaluate the tradeoffs of adaptation strategies across resilience, equity, and speed.
  8. #SectorPlan — Develop a societal-adaptation proposal for a specific sector.

Seams to watch (for the overlap/dependency pass)

  • Democracy cluster (overlap) — #DemocracyAndInfo (C1), #DemocraticTest (C3), #DemocraticAlignment (C4), #DemocracyAdapts (C5). Decide which course teaches democratic institutions vs. merely applies the concept.
  • Economy cluster (overlap) — #WhoControlsAI (Core, political economy), #EconomyAndWork (C1, as impact), #EconomyAdapts (C5, as reform target).
  • Alignment/eval dependency — Core #TrainingPipeline looks like a prerequisite feeding C4 (#CapabilityVsPropensity, #AlignmentToolkit, #ScalingLimits) and C1 (#MeasureNow).
  • Institutional toolkit boundary — #GovToolkit (C3) vs. #AdminState/#EconomyAdapts/#DemocracyAdapts (C5): where does "the tools" end and "adapting the institutions" begin?
  • Intra-course tension (C5) — #ClearThePath (a cross-cutting move) overlaps #AdminState (an arena), since clearing regulatory blockers largely happens in the administrative state.
