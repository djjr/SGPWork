MPP-AI — Gap Analysis (What the Courses Don't Say)

Companion to MPP AI Learning Outcomes (LOs). Question posed: if the mission is policy professionals prepared to make a difference in governing the A(G)I transition, what do the course descriptions and the 45 draft outcomes fail to mention?

Scope assumption (confirmed): the core MPP covers general policy craft (memo writing, quant methods, negotiation), so only AI-specific gaps count. Proposed outcomes below follow the DRAFT's conventions: verb-first, one cognitive action, #Tag handles, (candidate) where the case is contestable.

Part 1 — Structural gaps (missing whole territories)

Gap 1: International and geopolitical governance ⭐ biggest gap

The curriculum assesses geopolitics as an impact (#SecurityAndGeopolitics, C1) but never teaches the international governance toolkit. Every governance instrument taught (C3, C5) is domestic. Yet the defining features of the AGI governance problem — race dynamics between states and labs, compute as a controllable input, verification of agreements, the fate of countries with no frontier developer — are international. A graduate who can design a regulatory market but can't reason about export controls or treaty verification is unprepared for roughly half the field.

Proposed outcomes (as a new course, or a major C3 expansion):

  1. #ComputeGovernance — Analyze compute-based policy levers (export controls, chip tracking, cloud KYC, verification regimes) as instruments of international AI governance. (Seam: builds directly on Core #ComputeStack, which maps the chokepoints but doesn't treat them as levers.)
  2. #RaceDynamics — Model strategic dynamics among states and labs (racing, deterrence, coordination failure) and assess interventions intended to alter them.
  3. #TreatyDesign — Evaluate proposals for international AI agreements and institutions (monitoring, verification, benefit-sharing) against the track record of arms-control and nonproliferation regimes.
  4. #GlobalStakes — Assess how the transition distributes power and benefit across countries, including those with no frontier developers. (Extends #WinnersLosers, which stops at "populations and regions.")

Gap 2: The politics of getting anything adopted

The curriculum teaches students to design governance (#DesignGov, #SectorPlan, #NewMechanisms) but not to win. Nothing addresses power, interests, coalitions, framing, policy windows, or capture. C3's prose mentions "lobbyists/corporate government strategy" as a tool to survey, but no outcome makes political feasibility a skill. This is the classic failure mode of governance education: graduates who can draft the perfect mechanism and cannot move it one inch. AI sharpens the problem — the regulated industry holds an extreme information advantage and much of the relevant expertise.

Proposed outcomes (fit in C3, or a politics-focused course; see Part 3):

  1. #PowerMap — Map the interests, resources, and influence channels of the actors in a specific AI policy fight (labs, incumbents, civil society, publics, agencies).
  2. #CaptureRisk — Diagnose regulatory capture and industry–government information asymmetry in AI policy, and design safeguards against them.
  3. #WindowWatch — Analyze how policy windows open (incidents, elections, crises) and construct a strategy for moving a proposal when one opens.
  4. #AdoptionPath — Construct a feasible adoption path — coalition, framing, venue, sequencing — for a governance design produced in #DesignGov or #SectorPlan.

Gap 3: Deciding and acting under deep uncertainty, including crisis

C1 teaches forecasting (#Foresight, #ForecastAudit) — epistemics, not decision-making. Nothing teaches how to choose when timelines are contested, error costs are asymmetric, and some mistakes are irreversible. And nothing anywhere covers crisis governance: #EarlyWarning (C4) detects the alarm; no outcome answers it. If the transition is fast, graduates will govern in emergency conditions.

Proposed outcomes:

  1. #ActUnderUncertainty — Apply decision frameworks for deep uncertainty (precaution, option value, irreversibility, cost-of-delay vs. cost-of-error) to a contested AI policy choice. (C1 or C5.)
  2. #CrisisPlaybook — Design an incident-response mechanism for AI emergencies (frontier-model incident, large-scale misuse event): triggers, authorities, coordination, communication. (C5; pairs with #HardenTargets.)
  3. #ExpertDisagreement — Adjudicate among conflicting expert worldviews (fast/slow timelines, catastrophic/mundane risk) without collapsing into either deference or dismissal. (C1; deepens #ForecastAudit.)

Gap 4: The actual, existing legal-regulatory landscape

C3 treats "legal systems" at the theory level; no outcome requires knowing what already exists: the EU AI Act, US executive and state action, China's provisions, sectoral regulators, standards bodies (NIST, ISO), liability doctrine. Graduates will walk into offices where the first question is "how does this interact with current law?"

Proposed outcomes (C3, or split with C5):

  1. #RegMap — Map the current AI regulatory landscape across major jurisdictions and compare the mechanism designs embedded in each.
  2. #LiabilityGaps — Analyze how existing doctrine (tort, IP, privacy, antitrust, agency law) applies — and fails to apply — to AI systems and autonomous agents. (Feeds C3 #NewMechanisms: agent ID/registration is partly a response to these gaps.)

Gap 5: Present-tense algorithmic government

The curriculum is frontier-focused and skips the deployed present: algorithmic decision systems already inside government (benefits, policing, hiring), the documented harms literature (bias, discrimination, surveillance), and the accountability toolkit built for it (impact assessments, audits, procurement rules, redress). Two reasons this matters: many graduates' first jobs are here, and this constituency's politics shape what frontier governance is possible.

Proposed outcomes:

  1. #GovAI — Evaluate a government deployment of an algorithmic or AI system across procurement, impact assessment, audit, and redress. (C5; concretizes #AdminState.)
  2. #EverydayHarms — Analyze documented harms of deployed AI systems (bias, discrimination, surveillance) and assess the accountability mechanisms developed in response. (C1 or C3.)

Part 2 — Course-level additions (smaller, targeted)

Core — Building the AI Frontier

  • #LabGovernance — Assess corporate-governance instruments at frontier labs (RSPs/frontier safety policies, board structures, safety cases, whistleblower channels) as a layer of AI governance. (Could instead deepen C3 #SoftGovernance, which names the category but not these instruments. Given #WhoControlsAI, Core is the natural home: the labs are currently the de facto governors.)

C1 — Tracking and Anticipating AI Impacts

  • #EnviroFootprint — (candidate) Assess the energy, water, and land-use impacts of the compute build-out and the policy conflicts they create. (Prose says "every area"; this one is conspicuously absent and politically live.)

C2 — AI and What We Owe to Each Other

  • #InsiderEthics — Reason about role morality for professionals inside the transition: dissent, whistleblowing, complicity, and the ethics of working within institutions one believes inadequate. (The course covers what society owes; not what the graduate, in seat, owes. For this degree's actual alumni, arguably the most load-bearing normative question.)

C3 — Building the Governance Frontier

  • #TechPrecedents — Extract design lessons, including failure modes, from past attempts to govern transformative technologies (nuclear, recombinant DNA/Asilomar, internet, financial innovation). (#GovernanceRoots traces governance-in-general; the nearest analogical evidence base for AI governance goes unmentioned.)

C4 — Evaluating and Steering AI

  • Largely complete for its remit. One addition:
  • #SecurityOfModels — (candidate) Explain model security as distinct from alignment: theft of weights, jailbreak robustness, safeguards against fine-tuning away safety training. (A steering technique that fails against an adversary isn't a steering technique.)

C5 — Societal Adaptation to Advanced AI

  • #CrisisPlaybook and #GovAI (Part 1, above) both land here.

Part 3 — Program-level gaps (not fixable inside any one course)

Working with AI, not just about it

No outcome anywhere requires a student to use a frontier system. The curriculum studies AI the way one might study a distant volcano. Policy professionals who haven't pushed a frontier model to its limits will systematically misjudge both capabilities and failure modes — and will be out-analyzed by those who have. The GAIT memo's "AI upskilling salons" concede the point for DC professionals; the degree students need it more.

  • #FluentPractitioner — Use frontier AI tools critically in one's own policy work (analysis, drafting, red-teaming) and evaluate the reliability of their outputs.

Cheapest implementation: not a new course but a thread — every course requires at least one assignment where AI use is mandatory, disclosed, and critiqued.

Translation across the technical–political boundary

The degree's whole premise is that its graduates bridge technical and policy worlds — but no outcome names the bridging act. AI-specific (so not delegable to the core MPP): communicating eval results, capability claims, and genuine uncertainty to non-technical decision-makers without hype or false precision.

  • #TranslateTech — Communicate technical claims about AI systems, including their uncertainty, to non-technical decision-makers in briefing, testimony, and memo form. (Natural summative pairing: brief a mock committee on a #BuildAnEval result.)

Synthesis / capstone

Six courses, no outcome that forces integration. #Trajectory (C1) synthesizes within one course. Nothing requires combining technical judgment (Core, C4), normative argument (C2), institutional design (C3), and adaptation strategy (C5) on one real problem.

Part 4 — New-course candidates

A. "AI and the International Order" ⭐ strongest case. Carries Gap 1 (#ComputeGovernance, #RaceDynamics, #TreatyDesign, #GlobalStakes) plus the international slice of #RegMap. Without it, C3 must absorb four-plus outcomes and loses its shape.

B. "The Politics of the AI Transition." Carries Gap 2 plus #TechPrecedents and #TranslateTech — a course about power, persuasion, and precedent rather than design. Alternative: distribute into C3 (which then swells) and accept a weaker treatment.

C. "Governance Lab" (practicum/capstone). Client-based project or sustained simulation (crisis game, mock negotiation, mock rulemaking) integrating all six courses; #FluentPractitioner and #AdoptionPath live here naturally. Given the program's active-learning commitments, this is where the degree's promise gets cashed out — and it's the line on the CV that gets graduates hired.

If forced to choose one: A fills the largest content hole; C fills the largest pedagogical hole. B's content is the most distributable.

If you only add five outcomes

  1. #ComputeGovernance — the field's central international lever; currently invisible.
  2. #AdoptionPath — converts three design outcomes from academic exercises into policy skills.
  3. #ActUnderUncertainty — the decision-side complement to a forecasting-heavy C1.
  4. #FluentPractitioner — credibility with every future employer, and a corrective lens on all other courses.
  5. #RegMap — day-one professional knowledge no other outcome supplies.

Coverage notes (things checked and not flagged)

  • Misuse response: covered by #HardenTargets; only threat modeling is implicit.
  • Private/self-governance: category present in #SoftGovernance; only lab-specific instruments missing (hence #LabGovernance).
  • Interpretability, democratic input to alignment, state capacity, labor/distribution: present (C4, C5).
  • Moral status of AI systems: present as #DoWeOweAI (candidate) — keep it; C2 is the only place it can live.
