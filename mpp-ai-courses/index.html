MPP-AI

The MPP-AI Graduate

Artificial intelligence is not only a new technology, it ushers in a transformation of human society that challenges the very foundations of collective governance. The MPP-AI aims to educate the people who will shape the AI transition—graduating to positions in tech companies, in enterprise, in all levels of government, and all sectors of civil society. 

Our students will have a range of backgrounds, from computer science and engineering to law, the humanities and social sciences. The core MPP degree will instruct them in the foundations of government and policy, while this concentration will provide them with the conceptual, empirical and technical tools to understand and steer the AI transition. This will include understanding frontier AI systems themselves, as well as the nature and scale of their prospective impacts, and the many ways in which they require us to rethink the basic conditions of social life. And it will involve developing familiarity with the broad arsenal of tools at our disposal to shape those impacts for the better. This includes technical interventions in the systems themselves, as well a wide range of other governance techniques aimed explicitly at AI systems, to the many interventions that will be necessary in other social institutions in order to prepare them for both the shocks and the opportunities that advanced AI portends.  

Core Course

Building the AI Frontier: From Linear Algebra to Political Economy

Artificial Intelligence is already the driving force animating the US economy; its societal impact is likely to be orders of magnitude greater still. This course introduces students to the central concepts of artificial intelligence, particularly those underpinning the current AI transition. Students will learn about the different stages of training a large AI model, the paradigms within which rapid AI progress has been made, and the developments that may drive future advances. As well as developing a basic familiarity with the architecture of a frontier AI system, students will learn about their material foundations: the humans, hardware, supply chains, infrastructure and capital flows that make it possible to train vast AI models at an expense of billions of dollars.

Learning outcomes: TrainingPipeline · ModelAnatomy · ScalingDrivers · ComputeStack · WhoControlsAI · HypeVsReal · MathFoundations

Courses

Tracking and Anticipating AI Impacts

Current AI systems are affecting every area of the economy and society; still more capable systems in the near future may prove even more transformative. Tracking and anticipating these impacts requires drawing on a wide range of social scientific methodologies, as well as developing new ones for anticipating and steering what's on the horizon. This course will explore the current and prospective impacts—both opportunities and risks—of frontier AI systems across a range of societal sectors, focusing on themes such as AI's impacts on democracy and state capacity, on work, the economy, and quality of life, on critical infrastructure including our information ecosystem, on culture, family and relationships, on scientific progress, war and geopolitics, and the long term flourishing of humanity. 

Learning outcomes: MeasureNow · Foresight · EconomyAndWork · DemocracyAndInfo · SecurityAndGeopolitics · CultureScienceFlourishing · WinnersLosers · ForecastAudit · Trajectory

AI and What We Owe to Each Other

All technological progress changes the content of our obligations to one another, as new harms and benefits become possible; only rarely does technological progress change the grounds of those obligations, the reasons why they obtain. This course exposes students to the uncharted normative horizon exposed by rapid AI progress. They will learn to identify and interrogate both novel practical questions, like how courteous an autonomous vehicle should be and what actions or decisions can appropriately be delegated to an AI agent, and fundamental challenges about whether the decline of mutual interdependence undermines the foundations of the social contract that lies at the heart of the liberal tradition, or how we should think about the prospect of humans, in some sense, losing control of humanity's future. 

Learning outcomes: ContentVsGrounds · NewDilemmas · SocialContract · FrameworksClash · LongHorizon · MakeTheCase · DoWeOweAI

Building the Governance Frontier

In this course we will go back to the foundations of collective governing across the evolutionary history of humans to lay the groundwork for thinking about how the next phases of governance might be built to effectively ensure human flourishing through the AI transition. We will explore the theory of normative social order, markets, democracy, capitalism, socialism, and other methods of governance through human history. We revisit today's tools including legal systems, legal infrastructure, legislative bodies, courts, local communities, civic organizations, lobbyists/corporate government strategy, self-governance, market/industry organizations and standard-setting, and the role of epistemic communities. We’ll then explore innovative proposals for governance methods such as regulatory markets and the need for novel infrastructure for AI agents that link these agents to existing governance institutions. Our goal will be to lay the groundwork for true innovation in new methods of governance that are adapted to the scale, speed and complexity of AI while remaining true to the core ideals of democratic governance.

Learning outcomes: GovernanceRoots · WaysToOrder · GovToolkit · NewMechanisms · DemocraticTest · DesignGov · SoftGovernance

Evaluating and Steering AI

This course will introduce students to the technical tools used to steer AI for the better, focusing in particular on methods for evaluating AI systems' capabilities and propensities, and techniques intended to tune those propensities towards safety and societal benefit. Students will learn about evaluations that explore AI behaviors "in the wild" as well as more static evaluations in the lab, focusing in particular on "early warning systems" intended to expose propensities that, in more capable models, could be catastrophically harmful. They will explore the strengths and limitations of current "alignment" methods such as reinforcement learning from human feedback, and from verifiable rewards, and system-level defences such as input and output filters, and chain of thought monitoring. And they will explore early attempts to develop alignment techniques that could work for much more capable systems and that are more responsive to democratic values and processes.  

Learning outcomes: CapabilityVsPropensity · BuildAnEval · EarlyWarning · AlignmentToolkit · ScalingLimits · DemocraticAlignment · OpenTheBox

Societal Adaptation to Advanced AI

Technical alignment and other approaches to governing AI systems take the systems as their object, and seek to realize better societal outcomes by intervening on them directly. But transformative technologies transform everything; steering that transition requires intervening on other institutions. For example, AI has the potential to transform the administrative state, but in order to do so many of the norms and regulations that govern state action would need to be changed; powerful AI could transform science, but regulatory and other practices place roadblocks in the path of deploying transformative AI, those benefits will be foregone. And some AI risks, for example those associated with misuse, or that arise as an unavoidable byproduct of uncoordinated action by freely choosing individuals and organisations, cannot be mitigated by intervening on the systems themselves; our only recourse is to harden the potential targets against attack. This course looks at the government and policy interventions into other domains that can prepare society at large for the AI transition, focusing in particular on democratic institutions, the administrative state, and the economy.**

Learning outcomes: SystemVsSociety · ClearThePath · HardenTargets · AdminState · EconomyAdapts · DemocracyAdapts · AdaptationTradeoffs · SectorPlan
